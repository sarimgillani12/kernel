Kernel

A Short Film by Sarim Gillani

Cold Open

Falaq - The Splitting (of Khajoor)

EXT. CAMBRIDGE — DAWN

EXTREME CLOSE-UP.

Two hands hold a khajoor between them. The fingers find the seam. A pause. Then: it splits.

The two halves fall open. At the centre: a smooth, dense kernel. Smaller than expected. Containing, somehow, everything.

SILENCE — then the first sound: a bell from King's College Chapel, far across the river.

Cambridge wakes. A punt moves on dark water. A bicycle wheel spinning — classic green cycle. A library door closing at the top of Girton. Wind through the Backs.

"The conclusion of the philosopher is not the outcome of his reasoning.
On the contrary, his reasoning is the outcome of his conclusions." — M. Rafiuddin

Act I

Inteha - The Limit

INT. CAVENDISH LABORATORY — DATA ANALYSIS ROOM — NIGHT

The screen fills with a map of the ancient sky. Not stars — something older. The Cosmic Microwave Background: the universe at 380,000 years of age, its residual heat rendered in colour. Amber and blue. The oldest light that exists. A portrait of the cosmos in the moment it first became transparent to itself.

NABEEL, 24, sits before it. He has the still quality of someone who has spent years training himself to be still — and the restlessness of someone for whom that training has never quite worked.

His desk: equations on yellow legal paper, three empty cups, a physics textbook open and abandoned. In the top right corner of his screen: an email notification. He has not opened it. It has been there for two hours.

He pulls up a graph. A curve approaching a point. The singularity — the Planck epoch, the moment before which mathematics has no words. The curve terminates. There is nothing past it. This is where his thesis has lived for eight months, circling the same unanswerable question.

He stares at the termination point.

He opens the email.

We read it with him — close on screen, the cursor still blinking:

Nabeel — The faculty committee has moved its meeting forward due to Dr. Wexler's schedule. You now have until Friday at midnight to submit the final chapter, or I will be forced to defer your candidature for the academic year. Come see me tomorrow morning, 10 AM. We need to talk about what you're actually submitting. — Huw

Nabeel reads it twice. Then looks at the whiteboard covering one entire wall. Equations so dense they've begun crowding the edges, spilling into the border.

At the centre of the board: a gap. Four blank inches. Like a missing tooth.

INSERT — the gap in the whiteboard. White space.

He looks at his phone.

BABA. Three missed calls, across three weeks.

He sets the phone face-down.

He looks at the singularity on his screen until the screen goes dark from inactivity, and he is left looking at his own reflection in the glass.

He turns to the whiteboard. Picks up a marker. Stands before the gap. Raises it. Does not write.

He lowers the marker. Sets it down. Sits.

Outside: the first grey light appears in the window.

CUT TO:

INT. UNIVERSITY CENTRE PRAYER HALL — FRIDAY NOON

Jummah. Rows of men standing, then kneeling. Post-prayer: JUNAYD stands at the front, leading dua. His voice is measured, unhurried. Around him, students with raised palms.

At the back of the last row: JAMES. Hands raised like the others. But his jaw is tight. Something working behind his eyes.

Junayd reaches a passage about the oppressed.

Junayd

(in dua, quietly)

...and ease the suffering of our brothers and sisters in Gaza and Palestine. Grant them patience. Grant them relief—

James's hands drop. He speaks — too loud for this room.

James

This is escapism.

Every head turns. The Junayd stops. A hush that has weight.

James

(quieter, but continuing)

All these prayers, nothing changes. We need power. We need action. Stop putting it on God — where is God for the Palestinians?

Silence. James has surprised even himself. This came from somewhere below the place where decisions are made.

Junayd looks at James for a long moment. Then wipes his hands slowly down his face — the gesture that closes a prayer.

Junayd

(quietly)

Ameen.

The dua is over. Men begin to move. James does not move.

He sits alone in the last row as the hall empties around him. The overhead light hums. He looks at his hands. Open. Empty.

Act II

Envy of the priceless pearl!

EXT. CAMBRIDGE CENTRAL MOSQUE — EARLY MORNING — 5:50 AM

The city before it remembers itself. Mist on the grass. The mosque's architecture moving through its morning sequence as the sun begins. Beautiful arches, tree canopy, gardens like an invitation.

NABEEL walks with the purposeful blankness of someone who has not slept. He stops. He doesn't plan to stop. His feet simply stop.

He looks at the mosque's face. Then pushes open the front door and goes in, carrying his shoes in his hands.

He is surprised to see OMAR there. They register each other across the empty hall. Two young men from immigrant Muslim backgrounds, alone in a mosque before six in the morning. Neither there by accident. Neither there on purpose.

Nabeel

Such a pleasant surprise. Where have you been? What have you been up to?

Omar

(with a grin)

I was off doing Ice Dragon Boat Racing and snow ball fighting in China. Minus thirty degrees in Harbin.

Nabeel

WHAT. That is crazy. I have so many questions but the most important one is — were you not taking classes?

Omar

Hmm. Yeah. That's the thing.

Nabeel

What do you mean? What are you working on nowadays? Third year Maths, if I'm correct?

Omar

I am glad I met you. I'm going back to Bradford in an hour. My dad needs someone in the business. I've been here long enough.

Nabeel

You're quitting your degree? What happened?

Omar

I'm not quitting anything. I'm going home.

The distinction matters to Omar.

Omar

(after a beat)

Nothing happened. That's the problem. I came here because I loved mathematics — the actual thing. The shape of it. The way a proof feels when it lands. First year and a half it was still there. Then it went. I kept doing the work but the thing underneath it wasn't there anymore.

Nabeel

Could come back.

Omar

Maybe. But my dad's shop won't wait for maybe.

A beat.

Omar

Forget about me. Tell me about yourself. How come you're awake so early?

Nabeel

I never slept. Thesis submission.

Omar

The one on origins of the universe?

Nabeel

Yeah. I have until Friday midnight. I don't want to submit what I have.

Omar

And the one you want to submit?

Nabeel

Isn't finished. And my supervisor thinks it can't be finished. And he might be right.

Omar

Is he?

Nabeel

No. My framework is right. The geometry at the singularity — I've been approaching it spatially, like everything else, but if you treat the boundary not as a wall but as a—

He stops himself.

Omar

As a what.

Nabeel

An opening. A different kind of geometry on the other side. Not where space begins — where space begins to be possible. Pre-geometric spacetime. It's not provable by the usual methods. But it's consistent. And it holds together in a way the safe version doesn't.

He says this and then looks slightly surprised to have said it out loud, to anyone, in full.

Omar

So mathematically that holds — but it doesn't correspond to anything you can measure. That's your problem.

Nabeel

Yes. Exactly.

Omar

Submit the safe one.

Nabeel

You just said—

Omar

I said the framework is right. I didn't say you should blow up your PhD to submit it. Submit the safe one. Then in two years write this up properly and it'll change the field. That's how it's supposed to work.

Nabeel

The safe one is a lie.

Omar

It's incomplete. That's not the same as a lie.

Nabeel

It presents the boundary as the conclusion. The boundary isn't the conclusion. The boundary is where the question starts.

Omar

And what happens to the question if you lose your funding and have to go home?

Silence.

Omar

(quietly)

Some things have to wait.

Nabeel

Is that what you told yourself?

The question lands. Omar looks at him — not angry. With a directness that has something behind it.

Omar

Yeah. It is.

A long silence. Neither of them says anything more.

Nabeel

You'll come back.

Omar

To Cambridge? I doubt it. To mathematics — maybe. Eventually. In whatever form it takes.

He holds his hand out. Nabeel shakes it — and then, unexpectedly, both of them allow it to become something slightly more than a handshake. The grip held a moment longer.

Omar

(at the door — and then, a wink)

Submit the real one.

Junayd told me to find you.

You were loud.

(frustrated)

I don't know. Something.

Hassan

CUT TO:

Act III

Guide Light?

EXT. QUEENS' COLLEGE — THE MATHEMATICAL BRIDGE — DUSK

ESTABLISHING — the wooden bridge over the Cam. A perfect arc of diagonals and tangents, each timber set at an exact angle to the next. From a distance it looks self-sufficient. Like reason holding itself together with no need for anything outside of it.

NABEEL and JUNAYD stand on the bridge. Below: dark water. Above: the first grey of evening. A punt drifts under them, nobody on it.

Junayd

They say Newton built it without bolts.

Nabeel

That's not true.

Junayd

I know. But everyone wants it to be. Why?

Nabeel

(simply)

Because they want reason to be self-sufficient. Logic holding itself together with no need for anything outside of it.

A beat. The water moves below them.

Nabeel

And it can't.

Junayd

Is that why you went into physics?

Nabeel looks at the water. The question lands differently than expected.

Nabeel

(after a pause)

My father reads poetry. Writes it, when he can. He drove a taxi for eighteen years. Came here from Lahore with nothing. He read to me every night when I was small. Urdu, mostly. Things I was too young to understand.

A beat.

Nabeel

I chose equations instead.

Junayd

He was disappointed?

Nabeel

He never said so. That's worse, somehow. I think he hoped I'd bring both. And I only brought one.

Junayd leans on the railing. He doesn't rush to fill the silence.

Junayd

What's the progress with the gap in your proof? I made special dua for you today.

Nabeel

There's a section of the derivation I can't close. The mathematics on both sides of the gap are consistent. I know the answer is right.

A pause.

Nabeel

I just can't prove it.

Junayd

You know it's right.

Nabeel

I know it feels right.

Junayd

(carefully, not pushing)

And is that different?

Nabeel goes still. The punt moves further downstream and disappears.

Nabeel

(very quiet)

In my field it is. You need the proof. Intuition without proof is—

He searches for the word.

Nabeel

It's poetry.

A long silence.

Junayd

(no edge to it)

Is that the insult or the compliment?

Nabeel doesn't answer. But something shifts.

CUT TO:

INT. WREN LIBRARY, TRINITY COLLEGE — LATE AFTERNOON

The library built by Christopher Wren. A long white room above a cloister. Lime wood bookcases. Proportions so precise that speaking feels presumptuous — as if the room has already said everything that can be said.

NABEEL and JUNAYD walk slowly. A librarian at the far end. Display cases: the intellectual inheritance of this small city, behind glass.

Newton's own copy of the Principia — pages dense with his handwriting.

Junayd

(quietly)

He spent more time on theology than on physics.

Nabeel

I know.

Junayd

Doesn't that bother you? That the man who built your entire framework spent the back half of his life looking for something it couldn't contain?

Nabeel

He was looking for the same thing. The rule behind the rules.

Junayd

And did he find it?

Nabeel doesn't answer.

Junayd

You know what the gap in your thesis reminds me of? Wittgenstein. The man who traced the limits of language.

Nabeel

What did he say? At the end.

Junayd

He ended the Tractatus by saying: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."

Nabeel

And he meant that as a limit.

Junayd

He meant it as honesty. You reach the edge of what language can carry. And then you stop.

Nabeel

Or the silence on the other side of that sentence is the most important part.

Junayd

Not "there is nothing beyond what language can say." Just — language can't take you there. You need a different vehicle.

A long beat. The library around them — centuries of reason, every book a monument to what the thinking mind has built. And in the middle of it, two people who have, in their different ways, reached the same edge.

Junayd

Wanna walk towards the prayer room?

Nabeel

Sure.

(into camera, with total conviction)

Act IV

Mere Drop of Blood?

INT. NABEEL'S COLLEGE ROOM — VERY LATE NIGHT — 2 AM

He is at his desk. The new framework open on his laptop. He has been working for hours — the evidence: six more empty cups, eight more pages of calculations, two fresh notebooks opened and filled.

He stops.

He opens his desk drawer. A slim book — Urdu poetry, the spine worn to softness. He opens it, reads a page silently. His lips don't move. His expression does — a slow unfastening of something he keeps locked.

He closes it.

He looks at his phone. BABA. Three missed calls. He picks it up. Puts it down.

He gets up to make tea and sees, through his window, in the college common room across the courtyard: a light. Late for anyone to be there.

He watches.

CUT TO:

INT. COLLEGE COMMON ROOM — CONTINUOUS

JUNAYD, alone. The room otherwise dark. He sits at one of the long tables with a sheet of white cartridge paper, a bottle of ink, and a broad-nibbed calligraphy pen.

He is working slowly. Deliberately. Each stroke considered before it is made, then executed with the kind of commitment that has no undo.

What he is making is in Arabic script. Large, unhurried, deeply intentional. The strokes are architectural: each letter a structure, each word a building.

Camera holds on Junayd's hand moving across the page. The image slowly resolving. We will see it completed in a moment. For now we see only the making.

The door opens. Nabeel. Junayd doesn't look up — he's in the middle of a stroke. Nabeel sits in a chair across the room without speaking and watches.

The only sound: the faint drag of the nib on paper. The only light: one lamp over the table. Outside: Cambridge, asleep.

Nabeel watches Junayd's hand. The patient construction of something that takes this long, that cannot be rushed, that has no equivalent in calculus or proof — where the beauty of the thing and the making of the thing are the same act.

Junayd lifts his pen. Sets it down. Sits back and looks at what he's made.

Now we see it:

فَلَق

Falaq

The Splitting

The camera holds on it for three full seconds. Then Junayd looks up.

Junayd

(without surprise)

How long have you been there?

Nabeel

A while.

Both look at the paper.

Nabeel

What does it mean?

Junayd

The splitting. It's what happens when something that was closed — opens. A breaking, but more like a becoming.

Nabeel stares at the word. The expression of a man who has just heard described, in three syllables, the thing he has been building equations around for three years.

Junayd

I do this when I can't sleep. My grandfather taught me. He said writing is the only thing that makes thought physical. Forces it to commit.

Nabeel

Why that word?

Junayd

(simply)

You can't be the only one thinking about the start of the universe.

A long silence. The ink still slightly wet, catching the light.

Junayd

And this is for you.

He pushes the calligraphy across the table. Nabeel looks at it. Goes to say something, doesn't. Junayd insists silently. Nabeel accepts.

We just called it the olive tree.

I know.

HASSAN

No. It’s very important to not only argue but fight all the gods. Abraham did that. It's an old tradition. And then he was able to find his God.

(quiet)

We don't need more noise. We have enough noise.

And wish that when I join my family members, I am as successful as them.

JAMES

Ohh, they’re in Gaza?

HASSAN

They are not alive.

EXT. COLLEGE COURT — DAWN

The first light. Nabeel crosses the court with the calligraphy rolled under his arm. He stops in the middle of the courtyard, in the silence, and tilts his face up toward the sky.

The sky above Cambridge at this hour: not dark, not light. The exact moment between. The daily falaq — the crack of dawn, the splitting of night.

He stands there for a long time.

Then goes in.

Act V

Consult your Heart

INT. JUNAYD'S FLAT — EVENING

Junayd comes through the door with shopping. Sets a can of Coke on the counter.

Flatmate

Bro. Why would you get Coke?

Junayd

It was on discount.

Flatmate

Come on, man. You can do better.

Junayd

OK. You don't drink it. I'll have the rest.

Flatmate

Why? You don't boycott?

Junayd

I think it's a distraction.

Flatmate

How come?

Junayd

Look — I know where you're coming from. I genuinely care for the children in Gaza. But it's not like me not drinking this Coke will save them. I don't understand this entire boycott thing. So much energy spent, so little impact. Better to use that energy in ways that actually work. Make dua for them — that is something real.

Junayd

اِسْتَفْتِ قَلْبَكَ

CUT TO:

INT. JUNAYD'S ROOM — DEEP NIGHT

Junayd asleep. Still. His face in blue lamplight.

INT. DREAM — WHITE SPACE

No walls. No floor visible. White — the white of overexposed film, the white of something without origin. Silence that has texture.

A GIRL. Six or seven. Palestinian. She wears the clothes of a child who has been outside in weather she didn't choose. Her face is entirely still — not frightened, not sad. Something older than either.

She walks toward Junayd with both hands cupped in front of her. Like an offering. Like water she is carrying and doesn't want to spill.

She stops in front of him. Holds up her hands. He looks down.

In her palms: a single brass bullet casing. Spent. Still warm.

She holds it out to him. He takes it. She looks up at him with eyes that have seen the whole screen and the whole silence — and says, very quietly:

The Girl

Thank you.

The white floods in. The image bleaches out entirely.

Junayd wakes. His hand is closed into a fist around nothing.

He sits up in the dark. Doesn't move. The room returns around him slowly — the familiar objects of his real life. The desk. The books. The window. The city, asleep outside.

He feels as though a genocide was livestreamed directly into his sleep.

He gets up. Goes to the kitchen.

On the counter: the can of Coke. He stands in front of the fridge, in the half-light, and stares at it for a long time. The girl's face. The bullet. Thank you.

He stands there. He does not move. He does not drink.

LATER —

The flat, daylight. Junayd in the kitchen again. Getting water.

He opens the fridge. The can of Coke is still there. The audience holds its breath.

He reaches in.

He takes the Coke.

He drinks it at the counter. Alone. Looking out the window at nothing in particular.

He finishes it. Sets the empty can down. Looks at his own reflection in the dark window glass.

His face says: I am still asking the question. I have not resolved it. But I am here.

CUT TO:

Act VI

Manzil - Destination?

INT. CAVENDISH LABORATORY — HUW'S OFFICE — MORNING (10 AM)

Smaller than authority should require. HUW at his desk. He doesn't offer Nabeel a seat — Nabeel takes one anyway.

Huw

Tell me where you are. Honestly.

A beat.

Nabeel

The framework extension — I've made progress.

Huw

Nabeel.

Nabeel

It's not finished.

Huw

It won't be finished. Not by Friday, not by Christmas. What you're attempting would take another three years and a different kind of evidence than you currently have or can collect. I've been trying to tell you this for six months.

Nabeel says nothing.

Huw

What you have is a solid, genuinely original contribution on pre-Planck epoch modelling. It's a respectable thesis. You can publish two papers from it. You can get a postdoc. I have calls I can make.

Nabeel

And the singularity.

Huw

The singularity is a hard limit. You acknowledge it as such in the conclusion, note it as an open problem, and move on. That's not defeat — that's how science works. You work with what you can measure.

Nabeel

I have a new framework. It's not finished but the geometry is right. I can feel it.

Huw takes off his glasses and sets them on the desk. He is not impatient — he is doing something more unsettling than impatient. He is being careful with Nabeel, the way you are careful with someone standing near a ledge.

Huw

You can feel it.

Nabeel

The geometry. Yes.

Huw

Submit by Friday midnight. The presentable thesis. Or I defer your candidature and we revisit in October with new funding arrangements. Those are the options I have available to me.

He picks up his glasses. The meeting is over.

Huw

You're a physicist, Nabeel. A very good one. Work with what you can measure.

Nabeel stands. Leaves.

In the corridor outside, he stops and leans against the wall. Looks at his hands. Open. Empty.

LATER THAT AFTERNOON —

INT. HUW'S OFFICE — AFTERNOON

This time Nabeel doesn't sit down.

Nabeel

I'm submitting the framework extension. The full version. Not the safe chapter.

Huw sets his pen down slowly.

Huw

Nabeel —

Nabeel

I know it doesn't satisfy the conventional evidential standard. I know the committee will push back. I know you may defer the candidature. I'm still submitting it. Because the safe version presents the boundary as a conclusion, and the boundary isn't the conclusion. It's the beginning of the question. And if this department can't hold that—

He stops. He is not angry. He is completely calm in a way he has not been for eight months.

Nabeel

The framework is right. I can't prove it by the usual methods. But it holds. And the geometry at the singularity — the pre-geometric spacetime model — has internal consistency that the standard approach doesn't. I would rather submit something true and incomplete than something tidy and wrong.

A long silence. Huw looks at him — not the look of someone making a professional calculation. The look of someone remembering something.

Huw

The committee will not pass it as submitted.

Nabeel

I know.

Huw

You'll be asked to revise. It could take another year.

Nabeel

I know.

Another silence. Huw picks up his glasses. Puts them on. Something passes across his face that is private.

Huw

Have it submitted by midnight.

Nabeel nods. Turns to leave.

Huw

(quietly, not quite at Nabeel's back)

Dirac was told his first major paper was unprovable.

Nabeel doesn't turn around. He nods once — almost to himself — and goes.

CUT TO:

Act VII

Khudi - Self

INT. CAVENDISH LABORATORY — DATA ANALYSIS ROOM — NIGHT TO MIDNIGHT

Nabeel is writing. Not the careful, measured writing of the previous months — this has a different velocity. The kind that comes when you stop protecting yourself and simply go.

On his desk: the framed calligraphy propped beside the monitor. The CMB on the screen — the ancient sky. The oldest light.

He writes. He draws diagrams. He crosses things out and rewrites them better. He works through the night with a focus that is different from all the months before — not the closed, grinding focus of someone trapped, but the open, fast focus of someone who has finally decided where they're going.

Hours pass.

Clock on screen: 11:38 PM.
11:51.
11:58.

At 11:59 PM, he stops.

He looks at the document. Reads the final paragraph. Looks at the CMB — the amber and blue of the universe's first breath, the singularity that stands at the edge of everything we can know.

He looks at the calligraphy. Traces the letter with one finger. Falaq.

He hits submit.

The screen returns a confirmation. He closes his laptop.

He sits in the dark room. The CMB gone now. Just his reflection in the black glass of the monitor. Him and the darkness, which is not empty.

CUT TO:

EXT. COLLEGE COURT — DAWN

The first light. Nabeel crosses the court with the calligraphy rolled under his arm. He stops in the middle, in the silence, and tilts his face up toward the sky.

The sky above Cambridge at this hour: not dark, not light. The exact moment between. The daily falaq — the crack of dawn, the splitting of night.

He stands there for a long time.

He looks at his phone.

BABA. Three missed calls.

He presses call.

It rings. Twice. Then:

Nabeel closes his eyes. Breathes. A Tear sheds through his eyes

Nabeel

Baba

Nabeel holds the phone with both hands. The sky above him keeps splitting — the way it always has, the way it always will.

CUT TO:

EXT. CAMBRIDGE — PALESTINE MARCH — DAY

A crowd moving through the old streets. Banners. Chanting. Colour against stone. The city, briefly, changed.

JUNAYD in the march. He holds a sign: BDS. He has not come here by argument. He has come here the way things happen after long internal weather — not decided, but arrived at.

He moves with the crowd. Then stops.

Across the street, on the pavement, watching: JAMES. Alone. Not marching. Not walking away.

Their eyes meet.

A beat. Junayd raises his chin — a greeting, an invitation, a recognition. Come. Or don't. But you are not invisible to me.

James looks at him. His jaw tight. Something moving in his face.

Junayd calls across the gap between them:

Junayd

James.

A long beat. The march continues around Junayd, the city continues around James.

Then James — quietly, at a distance, in a voice that has something new in it — answers:

James

Yaqoob.

HOLD on Junayd's face — something breaks open in it, gently.

The crowd moves. The two of them: still.

FADE TO BLACK.
